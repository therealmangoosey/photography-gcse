01

What Photography GCSE Actually Is

Photography GCSE is not just about taking nice pictures. It is an art subject — assessed exactly like Art & Design. Your process, research, and experimentation count for as much as your final images.

The course sits within the art and design family of GCSEs. You will be developing creative thinking, visual communication, and showing a clear journey from a starting point to a finished piece. The two exam boards most commonly used in UK schools are AQA and Eduqas — ask your teacher which one your school follows, as the structure differs slightly.

Grade Breakdown 60% Coursework — a portfolio of work built across Year 10 and early Year 11 exploring themes you develop with your teacher.

40% Externally Set Assignment (Exam) — the board sends a paper in January of Year 11 with around seven themed starting points. You choose one, prepare for several weeks, then complete a 10-hour supervised session (usually across two school days) to produce your final piece.

Every piece of work is assessed against four Assessment Objectives:

1
Record

Observe and document ideas from your experience, imagination, and the world around you.

2
Develop

Experiment with ideas, explore the work of other photographers, and build on what you find.

3
Refine

Use skills, materials, and techniques with increasing control and intention — not just chance.

4
Present

Share a resolved, personal, and meaningful outcome that clearly communicates your ideas.

02

Why Start Preparing in Year 9

Most students walk into Photography GCSE on the first day of Year 10 knowing almost nothing. The students who do well are the ones who already understand the basics — aperture, composition, light — before the course begins.

Photography GCSE moves faster than people expect. Teachers introduce concepts quickly and expect you to apply them almost immediately. If you already understand how a camera works and how light behaves, you can focus on the creative and analytical side — where the high marks actually live.

The real advantage of starting early Year 9 is time to make mistakes without pressure. Take hundreds of bad photos. Develop your photographic eye — the ability to see interesting images in everyday life. This is a skill that takes months to build, and you can't rush it.
03

Camera Basics Before Day One

The Exposure Triangle

Every photo is made by light hitting a sensor. Three settings control how much light reaches it — change one, adjust the others. Together, these are the exposure triangle.

Setting 1
Aperture

f/2.8 = wide, lots of light, blurry background. f/16 = narrow, sharp front-to-back. One of your most powerful creative controls.

Setting 2
Shutter Speed

1/1000s freezes motion. Several seconds creates blur and light trails — exactly what produces the light painting work you saw above.

Setting 3
ISO

100 = clean, needs light. 3200+ = works in dark but adds grain. Keep it as low as possible.

Other Essentials

Focus — Choose which focus point is active rather than letting the camera decide. Manual focus is essential for macro and low-light work.

White Balance — Different lights have different colour temperatures. Understand it so you can control — or creatively misuse — colour casts.

Modes — Learn Aperture Priority, Shutter Priority, and Manual. Your teacher will expect to see you using these, not Auto.

RAW vs JPEG RAW holds far more data, looks flat straight out of camera, but gives you much more editing control. JPEG is compressed and ready to share immediately. If your camera shoots RAW, learn to use it.
04

Understanding Light

The word "photography" literally means writing with light. Two photos of the same subject in different light can look completely different. Learning to see light is the single most important skill you will develop.

Golden hour — the hour after sunrise and before sunset — gives warm, soft, directional light. Midday sun is harsh and creates strong shadows. Overcast days are a natural diffuser — perfect for portraits and macro work.

Front

Flat and even. Safe but can look uninteresting.

Side

Adds depth and texture. Usually the most dramatic option.

Back

Creates silhouettes or a glowing rim around the subject.

Top

Deep shadows under features. Dramatic or unflattering.

Bottom

Unnatural and eerie — rarely seen in nature.

Hard light (bright sun, bare flash) creates sharp, defined shadows. Soft light (overcast sky, softbox) creates gradual, gentle transitions. Neither is better — they are different tools for different intentions.

05

Composition

Composition is what separates a snapshot from a photograph. It is the one skill you can practise today, on any camera, with zero technical knowledge.

Rule of Thirds Leading Lines Natural Framing Symmetry & Pattern Foreground Depth Negative Space Fill the Frame Unusual Angles Visual Balance Colour Relationships

Rule of Thirds — Place your subject at the intersections of an imaginary grid, rather than dead centre. Leading lines draw the eye through the frame — roads, fences, rivers, corridors. Negative space gives subjects room to breathe and forces focus on what matters. Change your viewpoint — shooting low makes subjects feel powerful; shooting high makes them look small or vulnerable.

Colour — Warm tones (red, orange, yellow) feel energetic. Cool tones (blue, green) feel calm. Complementary colours like blue and orange create contrast; analogous colours like blue and teal create harmony.

06

Equipment

The camera matters far less than the person using it. A skilled photographer with a phone will produce better work than an unskilled photographer with an expensive DSLR. Every time.

Your phone is absolutely fine to start. Modern phones have multiple lenses and sophisticated processing. If you have one, you can start learning right now.

Your school will likely provide DSLR or mirrorless cameras, studio lighting, tripods, and editing software. Ask your teacher before buying anything.

If you want your own camera: A second-hand entry-level DSLR (Canon or Nikon, from the last ten years) costs £100–£200 including a kit lens. A 50mm prime lens (around £80–£100 second-hand) is an excellent addition — it forces you to move your feet instead of zooming.

Editing software Adobe Lightroom is the standard — free through many schools. Photoshop handles complex compositing. Free alternatives: Darktable (Lightroom equivalent) and GIMP (Photoshop equivalent) are both excellent. Start learning one before Year 10.

How the Course Actually Works

Here is the reality of what Year 10 and Year 11 look like — based on what schools across the UK describe to their students. Every school is slightly different, but the structure is largely the same.

Which exam board? The two most common are AQA (used widely across England) and Eduqas (used in many Welsh schools and some English ones). Both have the same four Assessment Objectives and the same 60/40 coursework/exam split — the main difference is in what themes come up in the exam paper and how coursework is structured. Ask your teacher which one your school uses.

Year 10 — What Schools Say

"Students will explore basic camera skills, darkroom, mixed media — and then produce an experimental individual response. In the second project, students focus on surrealist portrait photographers such as John Stezaker and Shirin Neshat before combining elements of their chosen photographers to produce a personal response."

The Heathland School, Hounslow

"We follow themes like 'Natural and Man Made' and 'My World' — exploring portraiture, location and studio photography, experimental imagery, documentary photography, photojournalism, film, and fashion photography. This gives pupils the opportunity to explore areas that show career potential."

Fernwood School, Nottinghamshire

"The minimum expectation is a finished sketchbook page per week. We have a range of cameras and industry-standard software in our studios. If you have access to a camera or camera phone at home, that will be extremely beneficial."

Fernwood School — advice to students

The pattern across most schools is the same: Year 10 starts with skills-building projects — you might do studio photography, darkroom work, camera skills, and artist study — then you move into your personal portfolio project where you develop your own theme with more creative freedom.

Practical reality At Tudor Grange Academy, Solihull, Year 10 begins with digital SLR workshops and manual technique, then moves to "'In the Style of'" projects where students take inspiration from specific photographers and produce responses. This is the model used widely across the country — learning through doing in the style of others before finding your own voice.

Year 11 — The Exam Unit

In January of Year 11, your school receives the externally set paper from the exam board. This contains around seven different starting points — broad themes like Symmetry, Celebration, Fragmentation, or Low Light. You choose one.

From January until the exam in spring, you work on your preparatory work: researching photographers, doing experimental shoots, annotating your ideas, and developing toward your final piece.

"Question papers are issued in January. The exam paper has approximately seven different starting points covering a range of techniques including digital photography, mixed media, fashion photography, culturally diverse photography, graphic design, and installation art. Students choose one question to develop over three months, then sit a ten-hour exam over two days."

King Ethelbert School, Kent (Eduqas board)

"From January, your focus needs to be on preparing for the exam — however, you may continue to make improvements to your coursework outside of lessons, which makes sense as it is 60% of your grade."

Photography with Miss Wilson — teacher resource

The 10-hour exam usually takes place across two full school days in April or May. You work in your art rooms and computer rooms. For the entire 10 hours, you are producing your final piece — the work you've prepared for over the past few months.

Grays Convent, Essex — a typical Year 11 structure Students begin a new project in January based on the AQA exam themes. They select photographers and artists for research and inspiration, produce a range of experiments and records of ideas, then work on their final piece during a 10-hour, two-day examination in May.

What Teachers Expect From You

"Spend at least two hours each week on homework. Visit London art and photography galleries. Use Photoshop to manipulate your images and develop your ideas. Learn how to control aperture, shutter speed, and ISO as soon as possible and implement this in your photography. Research photographers, create images in their style, write about how they created their work and give your opinion on it."

The Heathland School, Hounslow — advice to students
Take photos constantly

Outside of lessons. At home, on your way to school, at the weekend. The more you shoot, the faster your eye develops.

Annotate everything

Write about your decisions. Why did you choose this angle? What does this photographer's work make you feel? Annotation is a direct Assessment Objective requirement.

Attend extra sessions

Tudor Grange notes explicitly: "spending time outside lessons, including attendance in extra-curricular sessions, is an integral part of the course."

Photographers to Research and Study

25% of your marks come from artist research. Choosing the right photographers to study — and writing about them properly — is one of the highest-impact things you can do. These are the photographers most commonly referenced in AQA and Eduqas GCSE resources across the UK.

How to choose your photographers When picking artists for your exam project: "Do you really like their work? Do you have lots of ideas for your own work inspired by their style? Is the style and technique a suitable level of challenge? Are they a professional photographer with their own website? Do the two artists work well together?" — Photography with Miss Wilson (UK teacher resource).

Portrait & People Photography

Annie Leibovitz Portraiture / Celebrity

World-famous for powerful, dramatic portraits. Her work combines psychology with visual spectacle. A go-to reference for the portrait theme.

Nan Goldin Documentary / Intimate Portraiture

Known for raw, intimate images of people in everyday life. Highlights personal, often difficult moments. Referenced frequently for Identity and People themes.

Richard Avedon Portraiture / Fashion

Stark, clean portrait style — subjects against plain white backgrounds. Captures character and vulnerability. Excellent for the Human Form theme.

Cindy Sherman Conceptual Self-Portrait

Photographs herself in different costumes and characters, exploring identity and the construction of the female image. A favourite for Identity projects.

Shirin Neshat Identity / Culture / Portrait

Powerful black-and-white portraits overlaid with Farsi text, exploring Iranian identity and female experience. Used at The Heathland School, Hounslow.

John Stezaker Surrealism / Collage

Combines vintage film stills and landscapes in unsettling collages. Referenced by schools for mixed media and surrealist portrait projects.

Landscape, Place & Environment

Ansel Adams Landscape / Black & White

Iconic large-format black-and-white images of the American wilderness. The reference point for dramatic landscape photography.

Edward Burtynsky Industrial Landscape / Environment

Large-scale aerial images of industrial landscapes showing the human impact on nature. Referenced for Environment and Materials themes.

Fan Ho Urban / Symmetry / Light

Cinematic black-and-white images of 1950s–60s Hong Kong — dramatic light, geometry, and symmetry. Referenced for Symmetry and Sense of Place projects.

Michael Kenna Minimalist Landscape

Long-exposure landscapes with perfectly balanced, almost abstract compositions. Go-to for Symmetry and minimalist work.

Dorothea Lange Documentary / Memory

Iconic documentary photographer of the 1930s Great Depression. Her images carry strong emotional weight and historical resonance. Referenced for Memory and Documentary themes.

Street, Documentary & Social

Henri Cartier-Bresson Street Photography

The founder of modern street photography. Coined the "decisive moment" — the perfect fraction of a second that captures a scene's essence.

Martin Parr British Social Documentary

Satirical, colourful images of British seaside life and consumerism. Excellent for Connections and documentary projects. Very well known in UK schools.

Steve McCurry Documentary / Portraiture

Known for the iconic Afghan Girl — one of the most recognisable portraits in photographic history. Referenced for Emotion and People projects.

Alex Webb Street / Colour

Vibrant, layered street photography with complex use of colour and light. Referenced for Celebration and cultural photography.

Elliott Erwitt Street / Humour

Joyful, humorous black-and-white images of everyday situations. Known for photographing dogs alongside humans in unexpected ways.

Abstract, Experimental & Conceptual

Man Ray Surrealism / Experimental

Pioneered techniques like the rayograph (photogram without a camera) and solarisation. Ideal for experimental and alternative process projects.

David Hockney Photo Collage / Fragmentation

Created "joiners" — photographic collages that fragment and reassemble a subject from multiple viewpoints. Directly referenced for Fragmentation and Layers themes.

Eadweard Muybridge Motion / Time

Pioneered photographic studies of movement in the 1870s–1880s. Referenced for Movement and Time themes.

Idris Khan Layering / Abstraction

Stacks multiple photographs into a single image, creating layered, almost musical compositions. Referenced for the Layers project described in grade examples above.

Liam Wong Night / Neon / Urban

Cinematic neon-lit night photography, heavily influenced by cyberpunk aesthetics. Referenced in the Grade 6 Low Light Photography project described on the Examples page.

How to Get Top Marks

The students who get the highest marks are not necessarily the best photographers. They are the students who understand what examiners are looking for — and deliver it consistently. This page tells you exactly what that is.

The Grade Boundaries — 2024 (AQA)

These were the AQA GCSE Photography (specification 8206) grade boundaries for the 2024 examination series, published by Photography with Miss Wilson from official AQA data:

2024 Grade Boundaries
Grade 9Highest band
Grade 7Strong pass
Grade 4Standard pass
Grade 140 marks (raw)

Note that boundaries increase by a mark or two most years. Always check the current year's official AQA documentation.

What this means

The marks come from a combination of your coursework (60%) and your exam component (40%). Both are marked against the same four Assessment Objectives. Getting a consistent balance across all four AOs is more important than excelling at one or two.

The Most Important Habits — What Actually Gets You Marks

Annotate everything

This is the single biggest differentiator between grades. Write about every decision: why you chose this angle, what this photographer's work made you feel, what you tried and what didn't work. Annotation directly addresses AO1 and AO3.

Link to photographers constantly

Every experiment and every shoot should reference a named photographer. "Inspired by Martin Parr's use of colour…" or "Taking Cindy Sherman's approach to constructed identity…". AO1 requires demonstrated critical understanding of sources — 25% of your marks.

Show failed experiments

Do not hide work that didn't go well. Showing what didn't work and explaining why, and what you tried differently, is direct evidence of AO2 (Refine) and AO3 (Record). Examiners reward documented thinking.

Use a checklist

Get your teacher to give you the mark scheme criteria and stick it in the front of your sketchbook. Refer to it constantly. "I religiously followed these criteria sheets and was only a few marks off an A" — former student on The Student Room.

Present clearly

A clean, well-organised sketchbook can boost your grade. The examiner should be able to follow your creative journey easily. Good presentation makes your thinking legible — and legible thinking gets marked.

Sketchbook: one page per week minimum

Fernwood School states this directly as their minimum expectation. The portfolio builds across two years — if you fall behind, catching up becomes extremely difficult under the pressure of Year 11 exams.

How to Write About a Photographer — AO1

Writing about photographers is the part most students find hardest. Here is the structure used by Photography with Miss Wilson in their teacher resources — a reliable framework that addresses the Assessment Objective directly:

Photographer analysis framework "The photographer I have chosen is…"
"Having read about their photography, I have learnt that…"
"When I look at [photographer]'s photographs, I can see…"
"One of the reasons I have chosen this photographer is because I like…"
"I will take inspiration from their work by…"

The key things to cover in any photographer analysis: who they are and when they worked; what their subject matter or theme is; the technical choices they make (light, composition, aperture, colour); the mood or feeling their work creates; and what you specifically will take from their work into your own photography.

The last point is the most important — and the most commonly missed. You are not writing an essay about the photographer; you are documenting how they will influence your own work.

How to Write Your Final Evaluation

At the end of a project, you will write a final evaluation. Here is the structure from Photography with Miss Wilson:

Final evaluation structure 1. What was the title and how did you develop it? What approach did you take?

2. Which artists inspired your project and how? Be specific.

3. What did you experiment with? What techniques and materials?

4. What is the idea behind your final series? How does it bring together the best parts of the project?

5. How did you create your final images? Technical process.

6. Strengths and areas for development. Be honest — self-criticism shows maturity.

"No one-word answers. Explain yourself fully and expand on your answers as much as you can." — Photography with Miss Wilson

Preparing for the 10-Hour Exam

"You must consider the date of the exam a final deadline — you will not have access to your websites outside of exam times. From January, your focus needs to be on preparing for the exam."

Photography with Miss Wilson — Year 11 exam prep

The exam is not the time to have new ideas. Every creative decision should already be made during the preparation period. The 10 hours are for executing a plan you have already developed thoroughly. Students who walk in with a clear vision and detailed preparation consistently outperform those who improvise on the day.

Choose your theme carefully

Pick the theme you have the most genuine interest in and the most practical access to. You will spend three months with it.

Do two planning pages per artist

One page of drawing and planning for each photographer you're working in the style of — including diagrams of composition and lighting setups.

Do a mind map first

Before shooting anything, list every association you have with the theme. This is where ideas come from. Teachers often run this as a whole-class exercise at the start of the exam unit.
